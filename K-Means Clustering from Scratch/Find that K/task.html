Description

Congratulations! Now you have a working KMeans algorithm. However, simply implementing the algorithm is not enough, as it does require us to tune a major hyperparameter: k.

To do it, we suggest you run your coded algorithm with different k values ranging from 1 to 10 and find which k value is the most accurate. The easiest way to do that is to plot the dependence of the "error" from k. We would introduce the "error" in the following way: let us say that it is the sum of distances from each object to its cluster center divided by the number of clusters. You will see that at some point, the "error" stops falling drastically; that is when you need to stop.

Of course, there's no clear definition of when that "stop falling drastically" occurs. Suppose that the rule for this particular case is the error decrease of 20%. Once the error decrease is less than 20%, stop increasing k.

Try to determine the appropriate k on your own to compare it with the one we expected at the end of the project.

Objectives
  1. Use the custom KMeans class with k ranging from 1 to 10 (both included) and calculate the final mean square distance from objects of each cluster to the cluster's center. The "mean" is implied only "inside" of each class distance measurement; for the final sum use the sum, not mean.
  2. Guess an appropriate k for the data. It is clear that the more the value of k is, the less "error" will be present, but who really needs millions of clusters for their data?
  3. Print the results for the "error" for each of the k values.
